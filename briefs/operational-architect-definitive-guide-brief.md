### FAQ Section (5–6 items)
1. **What is the difference between an operational architect and an enterprise architect?** — Enterprise architects focus on IT system design and alignment; operational architects design the human, process, and technology systems that make business transformation executable. The OA works across the organization, not just within IT.
2. **How does an operational architect differ from a management consultant?** — Consultants deliver recommendations from the outside; the OA operates from within, owning outcomes. The engagement is defined by accountability for results — measured in revenue risk mitigated, cost avoidance achieved, and regulatory compliance delivered — not by documents produced.
3. **What is a Target Operating Model (TOM)?** — A TOM is the blueprint for how an organization will operate after transformation — defining the structure, processes, technology, and governance required to achieve the strategic vision. Unlike a strategy document, a TOM is operationally specific enough to serve as a Day 1 implementation guide.
4. **What is the Tri-Modal framework?** — Tri-Modal leadership describes the ability to simultaneously communicate across three organizational dialects: the Boardroom (Executive: value, risk, capital), the Blueprint (Strategist: process, architecture, data), and the Engine Room (Engineer: systems, code, latency). It is the core competency distinguishing an Operational Architect from single-mode specialists.
5. **When should a company hire an operational architect?** — The highest-value engagement windows are: pre-Day 1 of a major acquisition (to design the Integration Management Office and prevent the 70–90% failure rate), when a digital transformation program has stalled or budget is escalating without outcomes, and when agentic AI deployment is outpacing governance frameworks.
6. **What is the "Missing Middle" in enterprise operations?** — The Missing Middle describes the class of work that is too complex for operational support, too noisy for engineering escalation, and too tactical for strategic leadership — creating an accountability vacuum that generates cost, risk, and compliance exposure. The Operational Architect is the role designed to own it.
